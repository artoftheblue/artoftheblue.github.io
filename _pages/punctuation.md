## General Rules

### Homogenous Parts of Speech

Two homogenous words connected by ***and/as well as/but/or/nor*** do not require a comma:

* I do this and that.
* I do this but not that.
* I do this as well as that.
* I do this or that.

Three or more homogenous connected words all require separating commas even before ***and***. In other words, the oxford comma is used:

* I do this, this, and that.
* I do this, this, this, or that.
* I do this, this, this, this, as well as that.

The following is not recommended in formal English; however, it may be acceptable when conserving expression or writing informally. Absolutely ***do not*** use such tricks when writing formal texts as it breeds ambiguity: 

* I do this, this, this.
* I do this and this and this.
* I do this, and this, and this.

It is possible to pair words by meaning, then each pair functions as a single block. This block functions as a single homogenous part of speech. The oxford comma allows to correctly join the last homogenous word to the rest without creating ambiguity:

* I do all the awesome and cool, weird and unusual, and fascinating but terrifying things. 
  
If a single logical block contains multiple homogenous words itself and is joined by other homogenous to it blocks afterwards, then semicolons should be used to separate multiword phrases.

* I do this, this, and this; that, that, that, and that; and those and these things.

### Dependent/Independent Clauses

Two independent clauses connected with a conjunction should be separated by a comma.

* I do this, and I do that.

Two independent clauses not connected with a conjuction should be separated by a semicolon.

* I do this; I do that.

A dependent clause after the main clause should not be preceded by a comma:

* I do this because I do that.

A dependent clause before the main clause should be succeeded by a comma:

* Because I do that, I do this.

### Defining/Non-Defining Clauses

Defining clauses are not surrounded by commas:

* I follow the channel that does something specified.

Non-defining clauses are surrounded by commas:

* I follow the cool channel, which also does something funny.

### Introductory Phrases

Objects before the subject should be separated by a comma:

* In Autumn of 2023, I wrote this guide.

Commas before the subject may be omitted if the style is informal or there are less than 3 words in the object that preceeds the subject. 

* Today I didn't do anything really.

Introductory phrases should always be succeeded by a comma.

* Basically, I didn't do anything today.
* To sum up, everything is on fire.

### Bullet Points

Bullet points can go like this:

* This style is acceptable, and the preceding sentence ends with a colon.
* Here, we write whole sentences.
* The sentences are capitalized.
* Each bullet point ends with a period.
  
Alternatively, they can go like this:

* this style is also acceptable, and the preceding sentence ends with a colon;
* here we write partial sentences;
* the sentences are not capitalized;
* each bullet point ends with a semicolon;
* the last bullet point ends with a period.

### Hyphen (`-`), En Dash (`–`), and Em Dash (`—`)

There are three dashes in English. The hyphen, the en dash and the em dash:

* hyphen: `-`;
* en dash: `–`;
* em dash: `—`:

Compound adjectives/nouns all use hyphens:

* non-traditional;
* computer-related;
* eco-friendly.

En dash is the slightly longer dash: –. The en dash is used to mark ranges of numbers:

* In 2022–2023, there were a lot of events.

The dash to denote sudden change in English language structure, for emphasis, or for explanation. The en dash surrounded by two spaces is recommened to use because it is more similar to how punctuation works in Russian:

* I had a short trip – which I really didn't like – last weekend.

Alternatively, the em dash with no surrounding spaces could be used (not recommended because it looks to alien in comparison to Russian).

* I had a short trip—which I really didn't like—last weekend.

### Direct Speech & Quotes

`"These"` or `'these'` are not quotation marks. `This's` an apostrophe. `“These”` are quotation marks. `‘These’` are singular quotation marks. It is recommended to use double quotation marks because they are the most similar to Russian. Most text editors automatically enter the correct quotation marks.

Exclamation points go inside the quotation marks, with no comma afterwards:

* “Everyone is awesome!” they said.
* They said, “Everyone is awesome!”
  
Question marks suffer the same fate as exclamation points:

* “Is everyone awesome?” they asked.
* They asked, “Is everyone awesome?”

For consistency, it is more logical to include commas and periods inside the quotation marks, considering that it is a widely spread and more academically correct usage:

* “I'm quite okay,” they said.
* They said, “I'm quite okay.”

Starting direct speech with an em dash is not recommended but acceptable because it is rather European than English:

— Hello!

— Hi!

Sometimes, it may be necessary to denote names of speakers, then use a colon after the name of the speaker and do not use any dashes:

A: Hello!

B: Hi!

Only titles of composed works should be surrounded by quotation marks (books, games, plays, series, films, TV shows, etc). In this case, the ending comma/period goes after the quotation marks:

* You should have watched “Oppenheimer” and not “The Barbie Movie”. 

Names of magazines, reference books, dictionaries, central religious books should not be surrounded by quotation marks. Instead, they may be italized (minus the Bible, the Koran, etc.), but it is not necessary:

* *The Oxford Dictionary* is the most reowned dictionary in the world. 

Other proper names (companies, events, etc.) are not surrounded in quotation marks, nor should they be italized:

* I went to Talented People Summit yesterday.
* The Very Cool Company has, unfortunately, ceased its operations. 

In case of partial quotes, the ending punctuation should be inside of quotes. Additionally, articles or any other determiners should not be excluded from the partial quote:

* They expressed “their utter disappointment.”
* He said he wanted “an apple,” but it turned out he wanted an orange.
* She made a remark that I was “the best” student ever.

Direct speech or partial quotes inside of direct speech uses single quotation marks. Capitalization in direct speech is conserved:

* “Yeah, she said I was ‘a freak.’ Kinda crazy, huh?” she noted.
* He said, “My parents were like, ‘You would be grounded for life.’”

## Specific Common Mistakes

### However, Otherwise, Therefore

***However/otherwise/therefore*** are not conjunctions but rather introductory phrases, unlike Russian; therefore, one might frequently see the following (preceding these words with a comma is incorrect):

* One does this; however, other doesn't do it.
* One should do this; otherwise, other wouldn't be able to do that.
* One has done this; therefore, other can't do it.

As an alternative, just split these clauses into separate sentences:

* One does this. However, other doesn't do it.
* One should do this. Otherwise, other wouldn't be able to do that.
* One has done this. Therefore, other can't do it.
